Overview

Devalipi is a constructed writing system for Sanskrit, one of humanity's oldest and most sacred languages. As a tool in a traditionally oral-transmission based culture, Sanskrit's native script, if it had one at all, has been lost to time. It makes sense to provide a script which is especially suited for this ancient language. The purpose of Devalipi is to illustrate Sanskrit's highly structured phonetics and provide a self-revealing pronunciation guide via glyphs based on natural symbolism such as tongue position. Devalipi makes Sanskrit easy to learn and appreciate. It is an evolving personal project by Lyam Boylan which has taken place over the span of a multiple years, out of a devotion for the spiritual language itself.

Structure & Transliteration

The first thing to know about Sanskrit is that it forms highly structured multidimensional grids, in its grammar, and especially in its phonetics. This is in comparison to the apparently random list of letters and the many exceptions to grammatical rules that we find in English, for example. In fact, the name for the language itself, Sanskrit, can be translated as "that which is made perfectly structured".

Similar in sound quality but differing in position to Sanskrit's dental (lateral) approximant, Vedic Sanskrit had a retroflex lateral approximant, in addition to the standard retroflex approximant. It is the only exceptional consonant to the 'magic rectangle'. To reflect this engima, it is designed as a composite between the lateral and retroflex approximant of the 'magic rectangle'.

Classification

One way to classify a writing system is by observing how they represent their vowels and consonants. Alphabets treat vowels and consonants equally, giving independent letters to each. Abugidas, like Devanagari (the most common script used to render Sanskrit), give independent letters to consonants, but vowels depend on their preceding consonant via diacritics. Devalipi is unique in that it gives independent letters to both vowels and consonants (technically making it an alphabet), yet treats them very differently, by utilizing the 2D space in which they exist to symbolically fulfill differing roles (perhaps comparable to abugidas, in some ways). Hence, Devalipi almost exists in its own category, as a hybrid between an alphabet and abugida, for it certainly draws inspiration from both.

Symbolic Considerations

The following is a list of considerations which went into the symbolic design of various aspects of the script. This knowledge may be useful for the purpose of mnemonics and for appreciating the otherwise-hidden structure of Sanskrit.

Five places of articulation.

You may discover that there are basically 5 unique places for sound to be associated with, in the mouth. Ordered by position (back of the mouth to front), these include the throat (guttural sounds), soft-palate (palatal sounds), hard-palate (retroflex sounds), teeth (dental sounds), and lips (labial sounds). Sanskrit too follows this pattern, as it is perfectly structured around exactly these 5 places of articulation. In honour of this beautiful fact, all letters of Devalipi are based on just 5 glyphs. Subsequently, they are each designed to reflect the tongue position (or lack thereof, in the case of labials) used to articulate each row of sounds.

Glyph Description
Guttural g Base of the tongue bulging to the guttur.
Palatal j Middle of the tongue arching up to the palate.
Retroflex D Bottom of the tongue curling back in retroflex.
Dental d Tip of the tongue tapping the teeth.
Labial b Tongue flat; lips pursing.

In this orientation, the left, top, and right side of each glyph represents the back, ceiling, and front of the mouth, respectively. Vowels use this orientation. However, consonants do not, as they are rotated 90° clock-wise relative to this.

Vowels

Vowels run horizontally over the consonants, reminiscent of the shirorekha, the horizontal bar which connects the characters of a word, distinctive of the Devanagari script. They run tip-to-tail, representing the flow of the vowel space, divided by consonants and broken up by consonant clusters.

Each vowel is a cursive form of the corresponding base-glyph of the same position. Similarly, the semi-vowels (which can be thought of as half-way between consonants and vowels) are stylized in the same cursive way as their respective vowel, but oriented vertically like all other consonants.

Consonants

Consonants fall perpendicular to the run of vowels, representing how consonants are connected only via other vowels. Though they all follow the natural curvature of the tongue or lips, they generally contain multiple strokes intersecting at cusps or sharp angles, invoking the characteristically abrupt nature of consonants as compared to vowels.

Voicing

Since a consonant can either be voiced or not (which is particularly important for discerning between plosives, but applies to all other consonants), this information is conveyed via whether the consonant touches the vowel-run or not, as vowels express unimpeded voice. In the case of no vowels, the unvoiced plosives have a disconnected dot where a vowel would be.

Nasalization

Finally, a consonant can be nasalized. This information is denoted by a small circle near to where the sound is symbolically produced. The association between small circles (bindus) and nasalized sounds is inspired by characters in Devanagari like 'ङ' (the letter for the guttural nasal consonant), and 'ंं' & 'ँ' (the diacritics for the nasalizing anusvara and anunasika), which contain a bindu each. The roundness of circles also correlates well with the softness of nasal sounds.

Fricatives

Since fricatives are technically a kind of approximant, their design reflects this as being stylized in the same way as the standard column of approximants, but with and without aspiration and voicing, respectively. Dissimilar to unvoiced plosives, which contain a dot in the absence of a vowel to indicate they detach from the voice, this information is unnecessary since their aspirated flourish is enough to distinguish themselves from the voiced approximants.

Practical Considerations

Each letter is designed with ease of writing in mind, with each glyph comprising only of one or two simple strokes. They are stylized for aesthetic reasons, to be easily identified, and to facilitate the natural curvature of writing as especially exemplified in India.
