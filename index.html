Overview

Devalipi is a constructed writing system for Sanskrit, one of the most ancient and sacred languages. The purpose of Devalipi is to illustrate Sanskrit's highly structured phonetics and provide a self-revealing pronunciation guide via glyphs based natural symbolism such as tongue position. Differing from most Indic scripts, it would likely be most accurate to call it an alphabet, as the consonants and vowels are technically independent. However, the vowels and consonants definitely have special treatment relative to each other. Evidently, many elements take inspiration from abugidas, like Devanagari, for example. Hence, Devalipi can possibly be said to exist in its own category as an alphabet-abugida hybrid.

Practical Considerations

Each letter is designed with ease of writing in mind, with each glyph comprising only of one or two simple strokes. They are stylized for aesthetic reasons, to be easily identified, and to facilitate the natural curvature of writing as especially exemplified in India.

Symbolic Considerations

The following is a list of considerations which went into the symbolic design of various aspects of the script. This knowledge maybe useful for the purpose of mnemonics and for appreciating the otherwise-hidden structure of Sanskrit.

Five places of articulation.

You may discover that there are 5 unique places for sound to be associated with, in the mouth. Ordered by position (back of the mouth to front), these are the throat (guttural sounds, from Latin guttur), soft-palate (palatal sounds), hard-palate (retroflex sounds, named after the tongue's orientation when its tip touches said place), teeth (dental sounds), and lips (labial sounds). Sanskrit too follows this pattern, as it is perfectly structured around exactly these 5 places of articulation. In honour of this beautiful fact, all letters of Devalipi are based on just 5 glyphs. Subsequently, they are each designed to reflect the tongue position (or lack thereof, in the case of labials) used to articulate each row of sounds.

Glyph Description
Guttural g Base of the tongue bulging.
Palatal j Middle of the tongue arching up.
Retroflex D Bottom of the tongue curling back.
Dental d Tip of the tongue tapping (the teeth).
Labial b Tongue flat; lips pursing.

In this orientation, the left, top, and right side of each glyph represents the back, ceiling, and front of the mouth, respectively. Vowels use this orientation. However, consonants do not, as they are rotated 90° clock-wise relative to this.

Vowels

Vowels run horizontally, reminiscent of the shirorekha, the horizontal bar which connects the characters of a word, characteristic of the Devanagari script. They run tip-to-tail, representing the flow of the vowel space, only broken up by consonants.

Each vowel is a cursive form of the corresponding base-glyph. Similarly, the semi-vowels (which can be thought of as half-way between consonants and vowels) are stylized in the same cursive way as their respective vowel, but oriented vertically like all other consonants.

Consonants

Consonants fall perpendicular to the run of vowels, representing how consonants are connected only via other vowels. Though they all follow the natural curvature of the tongue or lips, they generally contain multiple strokes intersecting at cusps or sharp angles, invoking the characteristically abrupt nature of consonants as compared to vowels.

Voicing

Since a consonant can either be voiced or not (which is particularly important for discerning between plosives, but applies to all other consonants), this information is conveyed via whether the consonant touches the vowel-run or not, as vowels express unimpeded voice. In the case of no vowels, the unvoiced plosives have an unconnected dot where a vowel would be.

Nasalization

Finally, a consonant can be nasalized. This information is denoted by a small circle near to where the sound is symbolically produced. The association between small circles (bindus) and nasalized sounds is inspired by characters in Devanagari like 'ङ' (a nasal letter), and 'ंं' and 'ँ' (two nasal diacritics), which contain a bindu each. The roundness of circles also relates well with the soft quality of nasal sounds.
